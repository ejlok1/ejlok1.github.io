## Eight Years

The last post on this blog was April 2018.

I closed Episode 6 — a deep dive on GRU networks — with a note that I was taking a "long hiatus as the cold winter arrives in Australia." I meant a few months.

Eight years later, I'm back.

A lot has happened. Let me catch you up — and explain why now, of all times, felt like the moment to restart.

## What I've Been Doing

When I last wrote here, I was an individual contributor deep in Kaggle competitions and NLP experiments. Since then:

- I've spent the better part of a decade as a **Lead AI Solutions Engineer at DataRobot**, working across APAC — financial services, healthcare, semiconductors. I've managed 100+ production ML deployments and seen firsthand what it takes to get models out of notebooks and into systems that run at scale.

- I achieved **Kaggle Grandmaster** status — competitions in NLP, tabular, time series.

- I've built patient risk models for hospitals, fraud detection systems for fintechs, compliance auditing pipelines for financial services. Real systems. Real stakes.

- And most recently, I've joined **H2O.ai** as a Senior AI Engineer, working on the next generation of enterprise AI — which brings me to why I'm writing again.

## Why Now

The blog was called "New Paradigm" from the start. When I named it in 2014, I thought I was being a little dramatic.

I wasn't.

We are in the middle of a genuine paradigm shift — arguably the most significant one in the history of software. And I mean that in a precise sense:

**The programming model has changed.**

For most of software history, you told computers *exactly* what to do. Every branch, every condition, every output — specified by a human. Machine learning was a partial departure: you specified the objective and let the model figure out the mapping. But the model was still a passive component — a function you called.

What's happening now is different. **LLMs can reason. They can plan. They can use tools.** And when you string together an LLM with memory, tool access, and the ability to take actions in the world — you have an **agent**.

Agents don't just answer questions. They:

- Break down a goal into subtasks
- Select and call the right tools
- Observe results, update their plan, retry on failure
- Operate across multiple steps without human intervention

This is a different kind of software. And most organisations — and most engineers — are still figuring out what it means.

## What This Blog Will Cover

I'm restarting New Paradigm with a specific focus: **Agentic AI in the enterprise** — from first principles to production.

I'll cover:

- **MCP (Model Context Protocol)**: the emerging standard for connecting LLMs to tools, APIs, and data sources
- **RAG pipelines**: how to build retrieval-augmented generation systems that actually work at scale
- **Multi-agent architectures**: orchestration, memory, failure modes
- **Embedding models**: what's new in the BGE-M3, E5, and text-embedding-3-* generation
- **Enterprise patterns**: deployment, governance, compliance, cost management
- **Kaggle-to-production**: lessons from competitive ML that apply to agentic systems

And — importantly — I'll carry forward the old series. The TF-IDF, Word2Vec, LSTM, GRU posts are still here, now annotated with 2026 context. That lineage matters. You can't understand where we are without knowing where we came from.

## A Note on the Name

"New Paradigm" in 2014 was about the shift from traditional analytics to machine learning.

"New Paradigm" in 2026 is about the shift from passive ML to active, agentic AI.

The name still fits. Maybe it always will — because the paradigm keeps shifting.

See you in the next post. There's a lot to cover.
